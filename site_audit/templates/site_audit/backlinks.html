Anchor text

Anchor text is the visible, clickable text of a link. It usually appears in a different color than the surrounding text and is sometimes underlined. In most cases, link text tells the visitor what to expect if they click on the link. Anchor text is a component of every backlink. Aa backlink is a link from a page on one website to another. When a website owner links to your site, you get a backlink from them. In turn, if you link to another website, then they have a backlink from you.

Anchor text is an important part of SEO. Look at your anchor text and in most cases, you will see words containing your business name and/or terms relevant to your industry. Google sees backlinks as a vote for your website “authority” and the actual anchor text in the backlink provides Google with a bit more information on what the vote is regarding. For instance, if you primarily sell “dog beds” but all your anchor text is for the word “cat furniture” because you wrote a popular blog post on cat furniture, Google is less likely to rank you as high for the keyword “dog beds” than if you had a lot of backlinks with the anchor text “dog beds”. It is important to keep in mind that when people link to your website you don’t get to choose the anchor text. They may put click here or here is the link instead of using the anchor text dog beds. In fact, if almost all your anchor text is one or two keywords it might start to look a little fake to Google. It is better to have a mix of anchor text that is relevant to your products/services. Take a look at your competitors’ main anchor text. How do you compare? If you are manually link building, you might even get some ideas for additional anchor text!

Referring Domains

Referring domains is the total number of websites that link to the website you are researching. Backlinks are an important component of a website’s authority and the ratio of the total number of backlinks to referring domains can impact how much “authority” Google ascribes to a website. For instance, a website with 100 backlinks coming from 50 websites would have a ratio of 2 backlinks per referring domain. This can be seen as more trustworthy in the eyes of Google than a website with 1000 backlinks from 2 websites. There is no perfect ratio but when comparing your website’s backlink profile and authority to your competitors keep in mind that the number of referring domains is usually a more accurate measure of how many “unique” backlinks a website has earned.



Results

The number of associated webpages that Google found in its index when an individual searches for a keyword. This number is usually giant because almost every page from every website on the Internet is cached by Google. For example, imagine if you walked into a library and said “dog” andthe librarian instantly said we have 56,000 pages in our books related to, or containing, that keyword. The results would be 56,000. The number of results can be a bit of a “too general” flag but really isn’t super useful. We include it because everyone else does. One way we have used it in the past is to look at the results volume of the keywords you rank well for. For instance, “London Plumber” has 9 million results, while “plumber” has 6 billion results. If all your ranking keywords are under a billion results maybe stick with that and don’t go after keywords with 1+ billion results (if you have any better suggestions how to use “results” let us know).

Total Number of Backlinks

Backlinks are an important component of SEO. In simple terms, older websites with more backlinks will usually have a higher website authorityand rank higher in Google than new websites with fewer backlinks. Thinkof a backlink as a vote for a website! That said, Total Number of Backlinks does not always accurately reflect how Google sees a website’s authority. For instance, this number when reported in We, includes every backlink we have found pointing to a website. If a websitehas 1000 backlinks all from one website, we will report 1000 backlinks. This is not nearly as powerful in Google’s eyes as 1000 backlinks from 1000 different websites (1 backlink per website). Other factors that impact how important this number is include relevancy and strength. Backlinks from relevant websites matter much more than backlinks from other websites that have nothing to do with the target website’s industry. Last, the authority and strength of the website that is providing the backlink matters. A backlink from the homepage of Amazon.com is goingto provide a lot more power than a backlink from a brand-new website. As you can see there are many factors to consider when analyzing the total number of backlinks your website or your competitor’s website has pointing to it.

Website Authority

Website authority predicts how likely a website is to rank for keywords in search engines. Website authority scores range from 0 to 100, with higher scores correlating to more powerful websites. Website authority is logarithmic, meaning for example, it is easier to go from 20 to 30 than it is from 50 to 60. When using website authority as part of your SEO strategy, remember to focus on websites in your industry/location that rank for similar keywords. For example, a dentist in London should compare their website authority to other London dentists’ websites.

Ranking Difficulty

How hard we think it is to rank in the top 10 for a specific keyword. When selecting keywords to write about (i.e. in articles/blogs) look for high-volume + low-difficulty keywords first!



CPC($)

Cost-Per-Click is the price an advertiser pays “on average” to bid on Google Search results and get a click. You can work hard to rank organically, or you can pay Google to put you at the top of any keyword search. After thousands of “actual” CPC campaigns of we’ve found that average CPCs as reported by Google (and us), are rarely very accurate. That said, they are directionally correct and thus very useful! The higher the CPC, the more likely that ranking for that term has monetary value. For instance, if someone searches for “types of houses” in London they may be in the market to buy, but if you were a real estate agent in London, you’d pay a lot more for a click from someone searching for “1 bedroom London flats to buy”. Look at the CPCs for terms in your industry and it will help you find keywords that are worth a lot to your competitors! The trick to writing content for your business, and getting more traffic as a result, is picking high-value terms that you can realistically rank for.
